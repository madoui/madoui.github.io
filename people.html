PhD Student

Kevin Sugier

I study the epipelagic copepod Oithona nana (Copepoda, Cyclopoida) at anatomical and molecular levels. Copepods are the most numerous animals on Earth and play an essential role in the trophic chain and marine biogeochemical cycles. The aim of the study is, based on genomic and transcriptional data, to identify and understand molecular mechanisms that may play a role in the development, sexual differentiation and behaviours in copepods, and more specifically of a small epipelagic zooplankton non-model organism: Oithona nana. The study includes a series of analysis that will integrate molecular reference data, molecular data developed within the lab, transcriptomic data from public banks, but also an anatomical analysis on the constitution of the digestive and reproductive systems. From these data and from the knowledge acquired on O. nana, a comparative analysis between the proposed model and the other copepods revealed a gene explosion involving a particular and unexplored functional domain, whose a large number plays a role in the male. Protein-protein interaction analysis coupled with differential expression analysis between developmental stages (egg, larvae, juvenile, female, male) identified the male-specific role of these genes in self-proteolysis mechanisms, excitatory neurotransmitter synthesis and neurogenesis. This could be the first indication of suicidal behaviour in the male, whereas O. nana is described as an iteroparous animal.

Romuald Laso-Jadart

After a Master degree in cellular and molecular biology in Sorbonne-Universit√©s in 2016, I began a PhD in population genomics at the Genoscope. For this thesis, we focus on a copepod highly abundant in Arctic Seas, Oithona similis, by using metagenomic and metatranscriptomic data collected by Tara Oceans Polar Circle expedition. The first part of this thesis was to deciphering the weak structure of Arctic O.similis populations. Then, the core goal of the project was to quantitatively link Allele-specific expression (ASE) and natural selection at the population-level. ASE is a phenomenon characterized by a variation of expression of a heterozygous genomic site. We created methods to detect and to analyze ASE at the population-level, and were able to find loci being subjected to ASE and natural selection for the first time. Among these candidate loci, we found enrichment in genes involved in nervous system. The following of the work is decomposed in three parts: -Investigating copepods nervous system by immunohistochemistry -Expand these observations to different planktonic organisms -Understand the nature of the link between ASE and natural selection

Julie L√™-hoang

My main research interest lies in the adaptation of symbiotic coral micro-algae to environmental changes in the Pacific Ocean. Coral reefs represent a very large and richness sources of biodiversity. There are 30 millions people who are directly dependent of coral reefs, because they provide food and they serve as barrier to protect coasts against storm damages (Tara Pacific summaries & Hoegh-Guldberg et al. 2007). They also serve to export marine organisms and nutrients (Moberg et al. 1999). Coral organisms live in obligatory symbiosis with the micro-algae from the family Symbiodiniaceae. In this symbiosis, the micro-algae uses inorganic components of the environment to produce organic components by photosynthesis pathway, where 80 to 95% are used by the coral host (Weis et al. 2008); It also produces Micosporine-Like-Amino-Acids to protect host against U.V. In exchange, the coral host provides Symbiodiniaceae protection and an environment highly concentrated in nutrients. It was observed that modifications in environment induce mass coral bleaching events, which are linked to Symbiodiniaceae expulsion from coral organism (Hughes et al. 2017). These modifications are mostly linked to global changes like rising temperatures and ocean acidification but also local threats like pollution, over-fishing and coastal urbanism. There is a very high diversity of the micro-algae grouped in 9 clades based on 18S sequences and there are 432 known species identified by the ITS2 sequences. Symbiodiniaceae is found in many hosts and there are generalists and specialists micro-algae (LaJeunesse et al. 2018). Today, there is a large amount of genomic and transcriptomic data describing these micro-algae and their activities in different environmental conditions (Levin et al. 2017), but the understanding of these mechanisms at ocean scale remains a major challenge for the analysis of reefs evolution facing global warming (Aranda et al. 2016). In this context, the Tara Pacific expedition (2016-2018) organizes a vast sampling plan around 32 islands across the Pacific Ocean which makes possible the analysis of coral micor-algae diversity and activity at ocean scale. My main objectives of the thesis are: 1. To study the capacity of Symbiodiniaceae to adapt to different environmental conditions in the Pacific Ocean by doing transcriptional and functional analyses. The goal is to understand how Symbiodiniaceae influence the resistance of corals to bleaching. 2. To compare the Symbiodiniaceae composition within and around coral at transcriptomic scale, to study the capacity of Symbiodiniaceae to colonize bleached coral. The goal is to understand how Symbiodiniaceae survive outside from coral and also far away from coral reefs. 3. Then, doing a population structure of Symbiodiniaceae to do global analyses of variants from Symbiodiniaceae transcriptomes. In fact, the ITS2 sequences are not sufficient to construct population structures of the micro-algae. The protocols used in Tara Pacific expedition are identical which allow doing population structure.

PhD student

Lucia Campese

Whereas global ocean connectivity patterns reveal the importance of dispersal in determining communities in the sunlit ocean, our understanding of the relative role of selection, dispersal and drift in structuring the global ocean is still poor. The main aim of my PhD project is to bring together expertise in theoretical ecology, genomics and oceanography to explore the role of physical connectivity between oceanic regions in phytoplankton ecology and evolution, with a focus on diatoms. The investigation is conducted with particular interest in regions of high productivity (upwelling zones) and high latitudes (the Arctic Ocean and the Southern Ocean), with metagenomics, metatranscriptomics and environmental data provided by the Tara Oceans and the Tara Oceans Polar Circle expeditions.
